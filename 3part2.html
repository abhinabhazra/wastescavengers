Procedure

To divide the class into four groups. Each group should choose one of the case studies, discuss with their teammates, reserach more abut it and present it to the enire class.

 

How Does India Manage Its Waste?

Students in various classes were assigned a case study and requested to create well research power point presentations on their given topics. The next day, students were to present their research infant of their classmates. Each student was to go in-depth regarding the topic and present and convey the information in an engaging way with their peers. They answered questions related to their topics from the students and gave clear insight on the role of students in waste management. Activities related to these case studies were conducted, including quizzes and a Q/A session. It was an insightful indulgence into flip teaching where each student was left educated and more aware of issues in places in India and their own locality and what they can do to help.

REFLECTIONS ACTIVITY 3

 

Do you think the current waste managemen in your school is effective?

 
The school's waste management is highly effective as it separates waste into appropriate bins for recyclable and non-recyclable materials. This practice not only ensures proper disposal but also actively contributes to environmental sustainability, making the school a cleaner and more eco-friendly place.

 

Where does the waste go finally from your school bin?

 
From our school bins, waste is segregated. The biodegradable waste is used to make compost. The no-biodegradable waste is sent to the municipal corporation and landfills.

 

Why do we have waste dumps in cities?

 
Due to high population and growth rates in the cities and the urban lifestyle of people, there is a higher waste generation, not only in their houses but also outside the societies, streets, roads, gardens and bridges. Therefore we have waste dumps in the cities.

 

Where are the waste dumps usually located?

 
The waste dumps or man made mountains are usually placed in the outskirts of cities or suburbs, but due to the growing population, these waste dumps are coming near the residential areas.

 

What happens when the dumps or landfills fill up? What if your house was near a dumpsite- how would you feel?

 
When dumps or landfills reach their capacity, several issues arise. First, the space for waste disposal becomes limited, leading to the need for new landfill sites. Identifying suitable locations for new landfills can be challenging due to environmental concerns and community opposition. Overflowing landfills also pose environmental risks. Leachate, a liquid formed as waste decomposes, can contaminate soil and groundwater. Methane gas, a byproduct of decomposition, may contribute to air pollution and climate change. To address these challenges, there is a growing emphasis on waste reduction, recycling, and sustainable waste management practices to minimize the reliance on landfills and mitigate the environmental impact of waste disposal. If my house was near a landfill, I would not feel good because of the trash and the bad smell all around

 

What are the problems that are associated with the ways in which cities manage their waste?

 
Cities face several challenges in managing waste effectively. Some key problems include:
Overflowing Landfills: Many cities rely heavily on landfills, which can lead to space constraints and environmental pollution due to the decomposition of waste.
Lack of Recycling Infrastructure: Inadequate recycling facilities and public awareness contribute to a low recycling rate, resulting in valuable resources being sent to landfills.
Improper Waste Segregation: Inefficient waste segregation at the source hinders recycling efforts. Contaminated waste streams make it challenging to recover materials for reuse.
Health and Environmental Risks: Poorly managed landfills and incineration can pose health risks to nearby communities due to air and water pollution, as well as the release of harmful chemicals.
Limited Composting Practices: Inadequate composting facilities and low participation in composting programs contribute to the underutilization of organic waste for soil enrichment.
Illegal Dumping: Improper disposal, including illegal dumping and littering, mars urban environments and can lead to long-term environmental degradation.

 

Is our current waste management system linear or circular?

 
Our school follows a circular waste system as the school works hard to reduce waste, reuse things, and recycle as much as possible. They separate recyclables from non-recyclables, use reusable items to make less trash, and recycle to create new things. This helps keep the school eco-friendly and clean.

 

Why do you think waste is managed in those ways currently?

 
Our school's waste management adopts a "reduce, reuse, recycle" approach for environmental sustainability. Emphasizing resource conservation, pollution prevention, and energy efficiency, the system aligns with waste reduction goals and potential regulatory requirements. By separating recyclables, promoting reuse, and recycling materials, the school minimizes its environmental impact. The circular waste system also serves as an educational tool, instilling a sense of responsibility in students and the community. Overall, these practices contribute to a cleaner, eco-friendly school environment, reflecting a commitment to sustainable living and responsible resource use.

 

How does our current waste management affect our health and environment?

 
Current waste management practises have a negative impact on both health and the environment. Improper disposal pollutes the air, land, and water, releasing hazardous chemicals and pollutants. Open dumping and incineration generate pollutants, which contribute to respiratory and other health disorders. Landfills emit methane, a powerful greenhouse gas. Plastic pollution, particularly in the oceans, endangers marine life and enters the food chain, impacting human health. Inadequate waste management exacerbates climate change and environmental degradation, highlighting the critical need for long-term waste solutions to protect human health and ecosystems.

 

Do you think the problems in the case studies occur in other parts of the country as well?

 
Yes, these problems do take place in other parts of the countries as well.
