Activity 4

Segregating Waste And Composting

 

Procedure

To understand the benefits of sustainable waste management practices by learning about segregation and the process of composting.

 
 
 
 

Segregation And Composting

We conducted the composting activity near the school ground in a special shed prepared specially to compost our materials. We collected our browns and greens and separated them. We then used our large composting pit. Firstly, we added the layer of browns at the bottom of our pit. Next we added a layer of greens. With the help of our school gardeners, we added a little semi composted soil which was moist. Then we added another layer of browns and kept our compost at mixture in the pit. After approximately 4 months, our compost was prepared.

 

Segregation

Students collected the school's biodegradble waste in different containers. They also separated the carbon rich material from the nitrogen-rich material.

    REFLECTIONS ACTIVITY 4

     

    How does composting reduce waste?

     
    Composting reduces waste by transforming organic matter, such as food scraps and yard waste, into nutrient-rich soil conditioner. Instead of sending these materials to landfills where they contribute to methane emissions, composting allows for natural decomposition in a controlled environment. The resulting compost can be used to enrich soil, improving its structure and fertility. This process not only diverts organic waste from landfills but also reduces the need for chemical fertilizers, promoting a more sustainable and circular approach to waste management.

     

    What are the other benefits of composting?

     
    Composting diverts organic waste from landfills, reducing the amount of trash ending up in landfill sites. When fully decomposed, it creates nutrient-rich soil amendment that can improve soil structure, fertility, and moisture retention. Compost contains essential nutrients for plant growth, reducing the reliance on chemical fertilizers. It also supports the growth of beneficial microorganisms and earthworms, fostering healthy soil ecosystems and enhancing biodiversity.

    What can you do if there is more compost thn what your school can use?

     
    If our school has excess compost, we can consider launching a community outreach program to distribute it to surrounding people, community gardens, or farmers. Organizing composting distribution activities in collaboration with local environmental organizations or municipal authorities. Alternatively, consider forming alliances with nearby schools, organizations, or businesses that might benefit from the excess compost. Implementing composting workshops or classes can also help to enhance community knowledge about sustainable practices. Consider giving extra compost to charitable organizations or food security projects. Surplus compost may then support local sustainability efforts and boost community participation.

     

    Where can you get the earthworms from for the composting? Where can you learn more about composting

     
    You can obtain earthworms for composting from local gardening centers, online suppliers, or even by asking other local composters if they are willing to share some from their composting systems.To learn more about composting, you can explore online resources such as government environmental websites, gardening forums, and educational websites dedicated to sustainability. Additionally, local agricultural extension offices, community gardens, or workshops often provide valuable information and guidance on composting practices. Books on composting by experts like Rodale or attending workshops and seminars on sustainable practices are also excellent sources of knowledge.
