The Packaging Problem

Students decided to use waste paper in order to make paper bags which can be easily made at an individuals convenience. Paper bags are biodegradable and recyclable, which means they can be broken down naturally or turned into new products without harming the environment. Plastic bags, on the other hand, can take hundreds of years to decompose and can pollute land and waterways. Paper bags are surprisingly strong and can carry heavy items without tearing. They are also more resistant to moisture than plastic bags, which can become flimsy and tear when wet. Paper bags come in a variety of sizes and styles, making them suitable for a wide range of uses. They can be used for shopping, carrying groceries, packing gifts, and even lining trash cans. Paper bags are safe for food contact and do not contain any harmful chemicals. They are also less likely to pose a suffocation risk to children and animals than plastic bags. Many people find paper bags to be more aesthetically pleasing than plastic bags. They have a natural look that is often preferred for packaging gifts and other special items.

REFLECTIONS ELECTIVE 2

 

Think of a natural form of packaging, something like banana skin. Why do you think it evolved to be pacakaged this way?

 
The skins of bananas act as natural packaging, protecting the fruit from physical harm during shipping and handling. Their waxy covering keeps moisture in and prevents drying, retaining excellent fruit texture. Compounds on the epidermis, such as ethylene, influence the banana's slow ripening. Furthermore, the waxy layer and natural scent deter pests, lowering the danger of spoiling. Unlike long-lasting synthetic materials, banana skins degrade into organic stuff, demonstrating environmental sustainability. They reduce dependency on non-renewable resources as a renewable byproduct of banana growing, demonstrating nature's efficiency in creating eco-friendly packaging for bananas.

 

Can similar methods be employed in man-made packaging?

 
The campaign for environmentally friendly packaging is gaining traction, emulating nature's efficiency. Biodegradable materials, such as corn starch or sugarcane-based packaging, provide a sustainable option by decomposing naturally and reducing landfill trash. Compostable packaging decomposes into nutrient-rich compost, which improves soil health. Recyclable materials, such as plastic or aluminium, save resources and reduce pollution by being recycled into new goods. Reusable packaging, which is meant to be reused, minimises waste and expenses. Furthermore, environmentally responsible practises extend beyond material selection to include factors such as lightweight designs to reduce shipping emissions. Businesses that use sustainable packaging solutions position themselves for future success as consumer awareness rises.

What are some of the types of packaging that cannot be recycled easily? For example metalized plastic ( most packaging from biscuits to snacks is packed in a thin layer of plastic bonded with a thin layer of metal) how can these problems be addressed. Packaging with metalized plastic poses recycling challenges due to the difficulty in separating the plastic from the metal layer. To address this, innovations in packaging design are crucial. Research into alternative materials or technologies that facilitate easier separation and recycling is ongoing. Additionally, consumer awareness and proper disposal practices can play a role in minimizing the environmental impact of such packaging. Advocating for and adopting sustainable packaging practices at both the consumer and industry levels can contribute to a more environmentally friendly approach.
