The Biodegradibility Experiment

 

Procedure

To understand biodegradibility by setting up an experiment to determine the biodegradibility of different substances or waste items.

 
 
 
 

The Biodegradibility Experiment

For this activity the students arranged for 4 pots with soil, in each pot a different item was buried in the soil. These items included a plastic bag, paper, leaves and a pot with leaves that was kept damp with water. over the next few weeks, the students made several observations regarding the materials in the pots. The only pot that witnessed degradation of the material was the pot with leaves that was kept damp. From this the students made the observation that materials required damp conditions in order to decompose. In the pots containing dry leaves and paper, partial decomposition was observed, meaning over time under the right conditions, these materials could decompose. However, the plastic bag remained unchanged throughout the entire duration, showing how plastic could not be decomposed and how this could potentially harm the environment. It highlighted the importance of being mindful of what materials we should use and highlights the urgency of the issue of waste.

Why do we categorize waste?

One of the primary reasons we categorize waste is to mitigate its environmental impact. Different waste materials possess distinct characteristics and can pose various risks to the ecosystem. By categorizing waste, we can identify hazardous substances that may contaminate soil, air, or water sources, and implement appropriate disposal methods. It also plays a crucial role in safeguarding public health. Proper identification and separation of waste materials reduce the chances of exposure to hazardous substances that may endanger individuals' well-being. Waste categorization also helps facilitate resource management and recycling efforts. By sorting waste materials based on their recyclability and reusability, we can maximize resource recovery and reduce reliance on raw materials. Proper categorisation allows us to identify materials that can be recycled or repurposed, reducing the demand for new resources and lessening environmental degradation.

 

What happens when you mix different types of waste like biomedical and hazardous wastes? Would it have an effect on human health?

Different types of waste, such as biomedical and hazardous waste, possess unique characteristics that warrant their careful handling and separation. However, when these two types of waste are inadvertently mixed, the resulting combination can intensify the risks to human health. Mixing biomedical waste with hazardous waste can result in the dissemination of pathogens to a broader range of materials and substances. These pathogens can contaminate water sources, soil, and air, increasing the chances of transmission and infection in both humans and wildlife. Certain hazardous waste components, such as heavy metals and chemical byproducts, can further amplify the toxicity of biomedical waste. As a result, unintended exposure to these compounds significantly heightens the risk of acute and chronic health effects, including organ damage, respiratory ailments, and cancer.

What is biodegradable waste? How can it be used to improve plant growth?

Biodegradable waste refers to organic materials that can naturally decompose or break down into simpler substances over time, typically by the action of microorganisms. This waste includes items like food scraps, yard waste, paper, and cardboard. Biodegradable waste can be used to improve plant growth through composting. Composting is a process where biodegradable waste is collected and allowed to decompose under controlled conditions. Since compost consists of decomposed organic waste, it naturally contains a good percentage of nitrogen. Therefore, adding compost to the soil enriches it with the important elements for plant growth and development, such as nitrogen, carbon, sulfur and phosphorus.
