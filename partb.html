STRATEGIES TO CHANGE THE LOCAL WASTE MANAGEMENT SYSTEMS

 

STRATEGIES TO CHANGE THE LOCAL WASTE MANAGEMENT SYSTEM

Over the years, it has become increasingly clear that there has been an increase in waste management challenges, even at the local level. The essay explores the cause and proposes solutions through the Municipal Council of Ludhiana. Ludhiana produces 1,100 tonnes of garbage or solid waste every day, which ranks highest among the 13 Municipal Corporations in Punjab. Waste Management in Ludhiana is facing numerous challenges. The waste management situation is further added to by the sewer treatment plants malfunctioning and industrial waste polluting the main city drain.

Various factors such as financial, human resource, political, and logistical constraints influence the current waste management practices in Ludhiana. The city is grappling with a host of challenges when it comes to managing its waste. The lack of modern technology and equipment has made the task of waste disposal and management difficult and inefficient. Additionally, the city is running out of space to dump and dispose of waste, and this has led to the accumulation of garbage in many places. There is no integrated solid waste management program in place, which has made it challenging to coordinate efforts and achieve sustainable waste management goals. These factors hinder the successful implementation of solid waste management programs in the city. The municipal corporation of Ludhiana has implemented various solid waste management programs, but most of these have been unsuccessful. The situation calls for policy recommendations to address the administrative challenges and improve solid waste management in the city. Ludhiana also has a problem related to waste burning, as the residue of burnt garbage becomes a common sight on the side of the streets.​ The city faces challenges with unsegregated waste disposal, leading to difficulties in recycling and managing mixed waste efficiently.

The environmental and health impacts highlight the urgency for change. The improper disposal of ​biomedical waste and the failure to enforce waste segregation at its source have led to environmental problems and pose numerous risks to human health.

The lack of modern technology and equipment, insufficient land for dumping and disposal, and the absence of an integrated solid waste management program further emphasise the necessity for an improved approach to waste management. Inefficient waste collection systems contribute to traffic congestion, causing disruptions in the traffic flow due to collection vehicles. The city also experiences difficulties in maintaining civic cleanliness due to improper waste management practices, leading to littering in public spaces. The management of landfills poses a problem with concerns about overburdened landfill sites due to excessive waste accumulation. Ludhiana, known for its industrial sector, faces significant challenges in managing industrial waste. The manufacturing processes in Ludhiana produce a substantial amount of industrial waste, requiring specialized treatment and disposal methods. Being situated in an agricultural region, Ludhiana encounters challenges related to agricultural waste management, especially during harvesting seasons. Ludhiana faces unique urban waste challenges due to its specific demographics, localized industrial activities, and varying population needs. Water pollution in Ludhiana is caused by industrial discharge, untreated sewage, and agricultural runoff. Industrial waste containing various dangerous materials and chemicals is often released into water bodies without adequate treatment. Additionally, the city's sewage treatment infrastructure faces challenges, resulting in untreated sewage finding its way into waterways. Agricultural runoff, containing pesticides and fertilizers, also contributes to water pollution in the region. ​ Ludhiana's water bodies, including the Sutlej River, are heavily polluted, posing risks to both the environment and public health. ​Instead of taking garbage to the designated dump sites, the Municipal sweepers can be seen setting waste, including polythene plastic bottles and other plastic materials, ablaze. This leads to the release of harmful fumes in the air and contributes to the deteriorating air quality.​ There have also been several instances of fires in landfills, further adding to the woes of the people.

Despite awareness campaigns and other initiatives, the city has fallen short of its efforts to segregate dry and wet waste, both at households and secondary dumping points. Only about 250 metric tonnes of the city's waste is processed. This shortfall in waste processing exacerbates the challenges associated with waste management. The ​municipality faces difficulties in effectively processing and managing waste at the primary garbage dumping site. This struggle adds to the environmental and health consequences arising from inadequate waste management practices.

Ludhiana faces numerous challenges due to poor waste management practices, which result in a range of detrimental effects on the environment, public health, and the overall well-being of its residents.

The poor waste management practices in Ludhiana contribute to environmental pollution. Open dumping and burning of waste lead to air and soil pollution, negatively impacting the local ecosystem and contributing to climate change.

Inadequate waste management in Ludhiana has caused there to be a risk of health issues among its population due to pollution. Improper disposal of waste can result in the contamination of water sources and the spread of diseases, posing serious health risks to the residents. The increasing air pollution in ​Ludhiana due to garbage burning has resulted in numerous health problems for its residents. The surge in air pollution has led to a substantial rise in respiratory problems such as cough and breathlessness. Health experts have reported a significant increase in the number of people suffering from breathing issues, with the main targets being those above 50 years and below 20 years. The rise in air pollution is also resulting in more cases of viral infections and pneumonia. Additionally, there has been an increase in heart issues and hypertension among people. The pollution has particularly affected those with a history of respiratory ailments, weak lungs due to certain diseases, and post-COVID-related issues. These health concerns highlight the urgent need to address the pollution concerns in Ludhiana to safeguard the well-being of its inhabitants. Furthermore, the inefficient waste management system in Ludhiana puts a strain on the city's infrastructure. Overfilled landfills, clogged drainage systems, and littered public spaces diminish the quality of life for residents and strain the city's resources. Poor waste management also has economic consequences for the residents and the local government. It can result in increased healthcare costs, reduced property values, and decreased tourism potential, affecting the overall economic well-being of the city.

The repercussions of inadequate waste management extend to social aspects as well. Unsanitary conditions in residential areas can lead to social unrest and community dissatisfaction, creating a negative impact on the overall quality of life in Ludhiana. Garbage-filled streets make for an unsightly view and make traversing the city difficult. The smell of garbage attracts many insects, also leading to communal dissatisfaction.

Addressing the challenges arising from poor waste management in Ludhiana is crucial to safeguarding the health, environment, economy, and quality of life of the residents of the city. Implementing effective waste management strategies and raising public awareness about responsible waste disposal is essential towards mitigating these pressing issues.

The current waste management strategies implemented in ​Ludhiana include door-to-door waste collection, segregation of waste at source, composting of organic waste, and setting up of waste processing plants. Additionally, the city has been focusing on public awareness campaigns to promote waste reduction and recycling. There are also initiatives to involve local communities and organizations in waste management efforts. Door-to-door waste collection was started to ensure the systematic and regular collection of waste from households. This initiative was set up to prevent the indiscriminate dumping of waste and promote the proper disposal of household waste. The segregation of waste at source was promoted to encourage residents to categorize their waste into biodegradable and non-biodegradable components. This approach aims to facilitate the efficient management of different types of waste, which can be further processed or recycled accordingly. The composting of organic waste is another significant strategy, focusing on the conversion of biodegradable waste into compost. This not only aimed to reduce the volume of waste sent to landfills but also to promote the use of organic compost for agricultural and horticultural purposes, contributing to environmental sustainability. Furthermore, the establishment of waste processing plants played a crucial role in managing the city's solid waste. These plants were designed to process and treat waste through methods such as recycling, incineration, or converting waste into energy. This would lead to the reduction of environmental pollution and landfill usage. The city's emphasis on public awareness campaigns underscores the importance of educating residents about waste reduction, recycling, and responsible waste management practices. By engaging local communities and organizations, Ludhiana aimed to foster a culture of environmental consciousness and active participation in waste management initiatives.

The ​Ludhiana Municipal Corporation has implemented several waste management schemes that have faced challenges and encountered issues in their implementation and outcomes. One of the failed schemes was the attempt to introduce a ​micro plan for door-to-door garbage collection and source segregation. This initiative aimed to address the proper collection and segregation of waste at the household level. However, the scheme faced difficulties in execution, potentially leading to limited effectiveness in achieving its waste management goals. Additionally, efforts to develop ​model colonies as 'zero waste colonies' encountered numerous challenges which may have contributed to the failure of the scheme. The objective was to make residents aware of waste management practices, encourage the avoidance of ​plastic usage, promote the use of ​cloth bags, and emphasize the segregation of waste into wet and dry dustbins. However, the outcomes of this initiative did not meet the desired targets. The municipal council's initiatives to promote source segregation and door-to-door garbage collection in specific wards also faced obstacles, possibly failing these schemes to fully deliver the intended waste management benefits to the city. Understanding the reasons behind the setbacks of these waste management schemes can provide valuable insights for future improvement and course correction in implementing effective and sustainable waste management strategies. The ​Ludhiana Municipal Council encountered several significant obstacles in its efforts to promote source segregation and door-to-door garbage collection in specific wards, hindering the effective implementation of these waste management practices. One of the major challenges faced by the municipal council was the failure to effectively enforce waste segregation at its source within the city. Despite awareness campaigns and initiatives, the desired level of compliance with waste segregation practices was not achieved, impeding the progress of the source segregation efforts. The poor quality of bins provided for the collection of biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste also posed a significant obstacle. Inadequate or substandard waste bins hindered the proper segregation of waste at the household level, impacting the effectiveness of the door-to-door garbage collection and segregation initiative. The municipal council faced challenges in waste processing and legacy waste management at its primary garbage dumping site near Kakka village. The struggle with waste processing and addressing legacy waste issues contributed to the complexities of implementing efficient and sustainable waste management practices in specific wards. Additionally, the private firm responsible for garbage collection was cited for not properly segregating waste, leading to environmental problems. This further compounded the obstacles in achieving successful source segregation and door-to-door garbage collection in the designated wards.

There are around 40 open secondary dump sites. The municipal corporation has also failed to impose a ban on plastic carry bags in the city, which were banned by the state in 2016. The city is working to install static compactors at open dump sites under the Smart City Project, but the project has not been started at the ground level.

These obstacles significantly impeded the municipal council's efforts to promote source segregation and door-to-door garbage collection, highlighting the need for comprehensive solutions to address these challenges and enhance the effectiveness of waste management initiatives in Ludhiana.

One potential solution for improving the environmental impact of the dyeing industry is to upgrade existing sewage treatment plants (STPs) and common effluent treatment plants (CETPs) by increasing their capacity to treat wastewater. This upgrade could involve adding new technologies or equipment to the existing infrastructure, improving the efficiency of the treatment process, or increasing the volume of wastewater that can be treated. By implementing these upgrades, the treatment plants would be better equipped to handle the high volumes of wastewater generated by the dyeing industry, resulting in a reduction of the industry's overall environmental impact.

Shifting dairies out of the city limits would make managing waste more efficient and effective. Additionally, alternative methods of domestic sewage treatment, such as decentralized wastewater treatment systems (DEWATS) and bioremediation, should be explored.

To address water pollution in Ludhiana, the release of freshwater from the Sirhind canal into Buddha Nullah can be done to dilute pollutants. Furthermore, enhancing the waste management infrastructure is essential, and this can be achieved by establishing functional waste processing facilities and installing compactors at secondary garbage collection points for efficient waste management.

There should also be a focus on implementing and enforcing regulations for proper waste disposal to prevent illegal dumping and ensure regular monitoring of waste management practices. Additionally, it is important to address the issue of accumulated legacy waste, weighing over 21 lakh tonnes.

Public awareness and cooperation are also essential to improving waste management practices. Educating the public about the importance of proper waste disposal and fostering cooperation from the community to reduce dumping and littering in public areas can go a long way in addressing the issue.

To strengthen regulatory oversight, work should be done to manage the increase in illegal industrial units and ensure compliance with disposal regulations. Recycling activities to reduce the volume of waste and minimize environmental impact should also be encouraged and promoted.

There should be an emphasis on waste segregation at the household and community levels to ensure that different types of waste are separated at the point of origin, making recycling and treatment more efficient. Investments and promotions should be made to expand recycling facilities and infrastructure to handle various types of waste, including e-waste, plastics, paper, and organic materials.

Extensive public awareness campaigns to educate residents about the importance of waste segregation, responsible disposal, and the benefits of recycling would also be greatly beneficial. Strengthening waste management policies and their enforcement to ensure compliance with waste segregation, recycling, and proper disposal regulations is also crucial.

Innovative waste management technologies, such as waste-to-energy solutions or advanced sorting and recycling methods, can also reduce the burden on landfills. Collaborating with private enterprises for waste management initiatives, encouraging innovation, and fostering a sustainable waste management ecosystem can help take some of the workload off the government.

Creating specialized e-waste management programs to handle electronic waste efficiently, including safe disposal and recycling initiatives for electronic gadgets is essential with the rise of technology, and by extension, a decrease in e-waste. Involving the local community through volunteer programs, waste management drives, and community-led initiatives to promote active participation in waste reduction and proper disposal practices can instil a sense of responsibility and help further promote waste management policies.
